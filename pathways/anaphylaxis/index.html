Anaphylaxis — Australian GP / Urgent Care Pathway

Rapid recognition → IM adrenaline → airway/oxygen/IV fluids → repeat adrenaline → transfer & observation

Step 1 — Recognise anaphylaxis (treat first, confirm later)

Anaphylaxis is likely if:
  • Acute illness with skin features (urticaria, flushing, angioedema) PLUS
    • Respiratory compromise (wheeze, stridor, dyspnoea)
    • OR hypotension / collapse
  • OR hypotension / bronchospasm after exposure to a known allergen
Do NOT wait for hypotension or rash to appear — early treatment saves lives.

Step 2 — Immediate actions (ABCDE)

  • Call for help immediately
  • Lay patient flat (or sitting if respiratory distress)
  • Remove trigger if possible (e.g. stop infusion)
  • Call 000 early — anaphylaxis always requires transfer

Step 3 — IM Adrenaline (first-line, lifesaving)

Give IM adrenaline immediately — do not delay
  • Adults & children >12 yrs: 0.5 mg IM (0.5 mL of 1:1000)
  • Children 6–12 yrs: 0.3 mg IM
  • Children <6 yrs: 0.15 mg IM
  • Inject into mid-anterolateral thigh
  • Repeat every 5 minutes if not improving
Key rule: IM adrenaline is safe. Delay is dangerous.

Step 4 — Supportive management

  • Oxygen: high-flow via mask
  • IV access (2 large-bore if possible)
  • IV fluids: Normal saline 20 mL/kg (adult bolus 1–2 L if hypotensive)
  • Salbutamol: for bronchospasm (adjunct only)
Antihistamines and steroids are adjuncts only — they do NOT treat shock or airway obstruction.

Step 5 — Adjunct medications (after adrenaline)

  • Antihistamine (e.g. cetirizine)
  • Corticosteroid (e.g. prednisolone or IV hydrocortisone)
  • Glucagon IV if patient on beta-blockers and refractory hypotension

Step 6 — Refractory or deteriorating patient

Red flags:
  • Persistent hypotension
  • Worsening airway obstruction
  • Multiple adrenaline doses required
  • Repeat IM adrenaline every 5 minutes
  • Prepare for airway support
  • Early senior / retrieval involvement

Step 7 — Observation, discharge & prevention

All anaphylaxis requires observation
  • Observe minimum 4–6 hours (longer if severe or recurrent)
  • Prescribe adrenaline autoinjector (EpiPen / Anapen)
  • Provide written Anaphylaxis Action Plan
  • Educate on trigger avoidance and autoinjector use
  • Refer to immunology/allergy specialist
Biphasic reactions can occur hours later.
